How wars get justified (the simulation rules)
  • Each pair of neighbouring factions accumulates justification (tension) from six historical drivers: border friction (expansionists rubbing shoulders), holy rivalry (two high-fervor faiths in contact), resource envy (your neighbour sits on more gold deposits), opportunism (aggressive powers eye weak neighbours), old grudges (lost wars demand revenge — amplified by Honor), and land scarcity (when the map runs out of free soil, everyone gets twitchy).
  • Aggression lowers the justification a faction needs before declaring war. Diplomacy constantly decays tension, brokers alliances and earlier peace.
  • Education drives technology → stronger, richer armies. Industry drives gold and growth capacity. Militarism sets how much of the nation is under arms. Religious Fervor adds zeal in battle but breeds holy wars.
  • Wars end through exhaustion — peace comes faster with diplomats at the table, slower with proud (high Honor) losers, and dominant aggressors may refuse peace entirely and annihilate the loser.
  • Victory: control 66% of the world, be the last faction standing, or — if utopia breaks out — win a Golden Age by score after a long era of total peace.
